Project 1: Exploring Flight Delays

Diving into the world of airport delays to uncover patterns and insights.

Project One

Introduce the Problem

Air travel is one of the most widely used forms of transportation, but it also comes with one of the most frustrating experiences for passengers: delays. Delays can disrupt travel plans, cause missed connections, and lead to increased costs for both travelers and airlines. While delays are a familiar part of flying, the reasons behind them and the ways they affect travelers are not always the same across different airports. Some delays may be due to weather, others from airline operations or air traffic control, and each type of delay can impact passengers in unique ways.

Understanding delays is important not only from the perspective of passengers who want reliable travel, but also for airlines and airports that aim to improve efficiency and reduce costs. By identifying which airports are more prone to certain types of delays, or which ones manage disruptions better, we can gain insights into how the air travel system works and where improvements might be possible.

Central Questions

Are certain airports more prone to specific delay types? For example, northern airports may experience more weather-related delays in the winter, while southern airports might be more affected by thunderstorms.
Which airports handle delays more efficiently? This involves comparing how long delays last and identifying airports that minimize disruption versus those where delays tend to escalate.
What proportion of flights at each airport are delayed versus on-time? By examining delay rates, I can compare overall reliability and efficiency across different airports.

By addressing these questions, the project seeks to provide a clearer picture of how airports differ in reliability, the types of challenges they face, and how effectively they manage delays. This analysis can shed light on broader patterns in air travel and highlight where passengers are most likely to face disruptions.

Introduce the Data

The dataset I am using is the Airline Delay Cause dataset, which I obtained from Kaggle (originally published by the U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics). It contains detailed records of flight performance across U.S. airports, focusing specifically on delay causes and outcomes.

The dataset includes the following key features:

  • year, month – Time period of the data (e.g., 2023, August).
  • carrier, carrier_name – Airline codes and names (e.g., “9E” for Endeavor Air Inc.).
  • airport, airport_name – Airport codes (IATA) and full airport names.
  • arr_flights – Total number of arriving flights at the airport.
  • arr_del15 – Number of flights delayed 15 minutes or more.
  • carrier_ct, weather_ct, nas_ct, security_ct, late_aircraft_ct – Count of delay instances by cause (e.g., airline-caused, weather-related, air traffic system, security, or late arriving aircraft).
  • arr_cancelled – Number of flights that were canceled.
  • arr_diverted – Number of flights diverted to another airport.
  • arr_delay – Total minutes of delay across all flights.
  • carrier_delay, weather_delay, nas_delay, security_delay, late_aircraft_delay – Total minutes of delay broken down by cause.

This dataset is highly suitable for exploring my research questions because it not only tracks how many flights are delayed at each airport but also provides detailed breakdowns of delay frequency, delay causes, and total minutes lost. This allows me to analyze which airports face particular challenges, how severe those challenges are, and how effectively airports handle disruptions compared to others.

Pre-processing the Data

To prepare the dataset for analysis, I first loaded the CSV file into a pandas DataFrame and inspected its structure. This included checking the number of rows and columns, looking at the column names, and reviewing the data types to understand what information was available. I also checked for missing values to see where the data needed cleaning.

Next, I handled missing values. Since the dataset contains counts and minutes, missing values in numeric columns were replaced with 0, which is a reasonable assumption that no flights or delays were recorded in those cases. For text columns, I replaced missing values with "Unknown" to ensure that grouping and analysis would still work properly. This step ensured that no rows were dropped and that all calculations could be performed smoothly.

After that, I performed basic sanity checks. Specifically, I made sure that the number of delayed flights (arr_del15) did not exceed the total number of flights (arr_flights). This prevents unrealistic results and keeps ratios like the delay rate meaningful.

I then created new features to better connect the dataset to my research questions. I calculated delay_rate, which is the proportion of flights delayed out of the total flights, as a way to measure reliability. I also created avg_delay_per_delayed_flight, which represents the average number of minutes lost when a flight is delayed, as a way to measure efficiency. In addition, I used the existing columns that break down delays by cause (carrier, weather, NAS, security, and late aircraft) to calculate how much each category contributes to the total delay minutes.

Finally, I saved a cleaned version of the dataset as a new CSV file. This ensures that all later steps in the project, such as visualizations and analysis, are based on the same consistent and preprocessed data.
